35mm Aerial Photography

Analog photography from a unique perspective

Getting a film camera airborne

This is a Kodak 735, a fixed focus F/9 35mm film camera with a glass lens and plastic body made in Brazil for Kodak in the 1990s. It's very simple to operate with its motorised film advance feature, so it's very much just point and shoot with this camera.

I've mounted it via some 3D printed brackets onto an 8" FPV quadcopter, built from salvaged parts from old DJI mavic drones. To actuate the shutter, a 9g servo is attached via another 3D printed bracket, and controlled via a switch on my radio over a spare AUX channel.
It was actually a bit convoluted to setup due to the lack of documentation in Betaflight (the flight control firmware on this quadcopter), which is very much geared towards multirotors and away from anything with servos, so I'll outline the steps here in case anyone else encounters the same difficulties that I did:

  • SERVO_TILT off in the Configuration, you don't need the automatic stabilisation mode
  • Enable CHANNEL_FORWARDING in the Configuration tab
  • Ignore the CAM_STAB mode, disable it
  • Uncheck all the boxes under the Servos tab, they mess things up. The min/mid/max, rate & direction settings do nothing for this use case. The servo values will also not change later once your servo is setup.
  • Map your servo to the correct pin in the CLI: resource SERVO 1 A08 or whatever the correct pin is
  • By default, channel forwarding will map AUX 1 to SERVO 1, AUX 2 to SERVO 2, etc.
  • I want AUX 3 to go to SERVO 1, so in the CLI I run set channel_forwarding_start = 6 (it's 4 by default, referring to AUX 1), thereby mapping AUX 3 to SERVO 1, AUX 4 to SERVO 2, etc.
